
WIRELESS PERFORMANCE ANALYSIS AND CONCLUSION

1. Distance vs. Signal Strength
In all three cases, the received power (Pr) follows the inverse-square law (1/R^2). 
As distance increases, the signal drops exponentially. This physical 
limitation defines the effective communication range for each technology.

2. WiFi vs. Bluetooth
While both operate in the 2.4 GHz band, WiFi has a much higher transmit 
power (100 mW) and bandwidth (22 MHz) compared to Bluetooth (10 mW 
and 2 MHz). This allows WiFi to maintain a significantly higher capacity 
over longer distances (up to 100m) than Bluetooth (limited to ~10m).

3. Cellular Performance
Cellular technology uses a much lower frequency (850 MHz) and a 
significantly higher transmit power (40 Watts). This allows it to 
cover vast distances (kilometers), whereas WiFi and Bluetooth are 
physically limited to meters.

4. Capacity Constraints
Even though Cellular travels the furthest, its bandwidth per channel 
(200 kHz) is the smallest of the three. Consequently, its peak 
capacity (bitrate) is lower than WiFi's, illustrating the fundamental 
trade-off between coverage area and data speed.

5. Final Conclusion
The simulation confirms that no single wireless technology is superior 
in every metric. Instead, they are optimized for specific use cases: 
Cellular for wide-area mobility, WiFi for high-speed local networking, 
and Bluetooth for short-range, low-power connectivity. The performance 
of each is strictly governed by the Friis Equation for propagation 
and the Shannon-Hartley Theorem for data limits.
